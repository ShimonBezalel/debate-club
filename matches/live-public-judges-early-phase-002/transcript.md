## pro_opening

The central burden is to demonstrate that public judge panels—where all participants see the judging criteria and often the judges themselves—are more valuable than hidden panels during the early phase of Debate Club’s development. This phase is characterized by exploration, learning, and iterative improvement rather than finalized competition.

Public judge panels enhance reproducibility and debugging by making evaluation transparent. When judges’ decisions and criteria are visible, participants can understand precisely why arguments succeed or fail, enabling targeted refinement of techniques and clearer identification of bugs or misalignments in argument models. This transparency reduces overfitting to obscure or idiosyncratic judge preferences, as participants can generalize better to the known standards rather than gaming hidden, unpredictable criteria.

While hidden panels may reduce gaming in mature leagues by preventing strategic tailoring, in the early phase this risk is outweighed by the need for open feedback loops that accelerate learning and innovation. Public panels foster community trust and collective calibration, essential for building robust evaluation metrics.

We concede that once the evaluation criteria stabilize and the community matures, hidden panels might better prevent strategic manipulation. However, evidence justifying a shift would include demonstrated convergence on stable, well-understood judging standards and a plateau in learning gains from transparency.

In sum, public judge panels maximize early-phase value by promoting transparency, reproducibility, and generalizable learning.
